Commuting in New York is safe, predictable and dull.

Unless you're on the roof.

Yo WTF!? These people just came over the Williamsburg bridge on top of the train. pic.twitter.com/osEtX4a0cp

— GOOSE (@GooseyMane) June 11, 2022

These roof-riders are known as 'subway surfers', mostly teenage boys, looking for adventure.

Their numbers skyrocketed in 2022 - MTA cases jumping 350% from 2021.

And they're in danger.

Social media must be socially responsible. Subway Surfing kills. We need everyone to be a part of ending this dangerous threat. pic.twitter.com/2RcEgEouLo

— Mayor Eric Adams (@NYCMayor) June 26, 2023

The subway is not a playground - boys as young as 14 have lost eyes, limbs and heads.

So why are they doing it?










Teenage surfers upload videos of themselves surfing on TikTok, in the style of the game "Subway Surfers", inspiring others.

The game revels in mischief...

...disregarding authority figures and safety rules...

...characters run along rails and train rooves...

...urged by the game to 'DASH as fast as you can! DODGE the oncoming trains!

You don't have to look far on YouTube for videos making it look cool.











Reality is less forgiving.

Deaths have surged beyond their 2016 peak…

...to five this year so far, mainly boys of 14 and 15 years old.













Here's a map of serious incidents involving surfers over the past decade.

Although they're pretty spaced out, there are two clusters around Delancey Street/Essex Street…

…and 4th Avenue-9th Street.





















Delancey is close to the Williamsburg Bridge, open air with spectacular views of Manhattan.




But the bridge is spanned by low-hanging girders, with trains passing close to each other.

One of the boys died after being hit on the head by something, the others falling onto the opposite track.





















The same is true on 4th Avenue-9th Street...

…the fine outdoor views while crossing over the Gowanus Canal…

…the girders…

…before leading into a tunnel.

All three casualties were hit in the head.

One of these was Christopher Serrano, a respected photographer on Instagram who shot photos of himself in dangerous places around the city.

Serrano was struck while trying to climb onto the roof with his girlfriend. He was 25.

At his wake, his brother said his favorite quote was: 'Life is not measured by the number of breaths we take but by the moments that take our breath away.'

In a nutshell: some people are so busy looking out for breath-taking moments, that they're not looking where they're going.
